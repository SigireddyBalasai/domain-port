Choosing between **4MP and 1080p** is the most common resolution decision for a new CCTV system. Both are mainstream, affordable, and supported by every modern NVR — but they are not interchangeable. 1080p (2MP) is enough for close, well-lit indoor areas, while 4MP (1440p) gives you extra reach and detail for entrances, driveways, and open rooms without the storage cost of 4K.

## The quick answer

| Spec | 1080p (2MP) | 4MP (1440p) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pixels | 1920 × 1080 | 2560 × 1440 |
| Total megapixels | ~2.1 MP | ~4.1 MP |
| Face ID distance (DORI) | 15-25 ft | 20-35 ft |
| Relative storage | 1× (baseline) | ~1.3-1.4× |
| Best for | Indoor, close range | Entrances, general coverage |

If your budget allows, **default to 4MP** for outdoor and perimeter cameras and use 1080p only for constrained indoor spaces. The detail gain is real and the storage penalty is mild.

## Pixel count and what it means

1080p captures 1920 × 1080 = about 2.1 million pixels. 4MP captures 2560 × 1440 = about 4.1 million pixels. The extra ~2 million pixels are mostly vertical and horizontal detail: you can read a license plate or a face label from farther away, and digital zoom stays usable instead of turning into a blur.

This matters most when an incident happens at the edge of the frame. A 1080p camera that "sees" a person at 30 feet may not resolve their face; a 4MP camera at the same spot usually will.

## Identification distance (DORI)

Surveillance cameras are rated with **DORI** (Detect, Observe, Recognize, Identify) distances. For a typical 4mm lens:

- **1080p:** identify a face at roughly 15-25 feet.
- **4MP:** identify a face at roughly 20-35 feet.

So 4MP effectively extends your useful identification range by about 30-40% for the same lens and mounting height. If your cameras cover a pathway or parking area where subjects are 20+ feet away, 4MP is the safer choice.

## Low-light performance

Resolution and night vision are different things. Low-light quality depends on **sensor size and pixel size**, not megapixel count. On an identical sensor, 1080p has larger pixels than 4MP, so it collects more light per pixel and usually produces cleaner night footage.

Practical guidance:

- Dark indoor hallway with no extra light → a **Starlight 1080p** camera often beats a basic 4MP camera.
- Covered porch or lit driveway → **4MP** is the better all-rounder.
- Always check the sensor size (1/2.8", 1/1.8", etc.) and look for **Starlight** or **ColorVu** technology rather than trusting the megapixel number alone.

## Storage and bandwidth

4MP records about 30-40% more data than 1080p. As a rule of thumb with H.265 compression:

- 1080p continuous: ~12-18 GB/day per camera.
- 4MP continuous: ~18-28 GB/day per camera.

For a system of eight cameras recording 24/7, that difference is roughly 50-80 GB/day — enough to matter when sizing an NVR hard drive. Enabling **motion-only recording** cuts both figures by 60-70%, which narrows the gap considerably.

## How to choose per location

- **Front door / entrance:** 4MP. You want to resolve faces and packages clearly.
- **Driveway / street-facing:** 4MP, ideally with a Starlight sensor for night.
- **Living room / small office:** 1080p is plenty at close range.
- **Warehouse aisle or retail floor:** 4MP for wider coverage and detail.
- **Hallway / stairwell (poorly lit):** a Starlight 1080p camera.
