CCTV installation isn't just about hardware placement and wiring—it's also about navigating a complex patchwork of privacy laws that vary from state to state. What's perfectly legal in Texas could expose you to liability in California or Illinois.

For installers, this means **every project needs a legal check** before you mount that camera. Missteps can lead to civil lawsuits, statutory damages, and even criminal penalties for willful violations. Your clients trust your expertise; that includes knowing where the legal boundaries are.

This guide outlines the key state-by-state rules for video and audio surveillance. **This is not legal advice**—always consult with counsel when in doubt, especially for commercial or multi-state projects.

## Federal vs State: The Legal Landscape

Unlike other industries, there is **no comprehensive federal CCTV law** that governs private surveillance. Instead, states regulate video and audio recording through:

- **Wiretap/eavesdropping statutes** (audio recording)
- **Invasion of privacy laws** (video in private areas)
- **Employer-employee surveillance rules**
- **Signage and notice requirements**

The federal government does regulate:
- Interstate surveillance (federal crimes)
- Video voyeurism (federal protections against hidden cameras in private spaces)
- Sector-specific rules (e.g., HIPAA for healthcare, FERPA for schools)

But day-to-day, **state law is what matters** for most residential and commercial installations.

## General Rules That Apply in Most States

Before diving into state specifics, several principles are widely shared:

### Prohibited Areas

Most states prohibit surveillance in places where a reasonable person expects privacy:

<Callout type="warning">
  **Absolutely avoid installing cameras:**
  - Bathrooms and restrooms
  - Locker rooms and changing areas
  - Bedrooms (in residential settings unless all occupants consent)
  - Any area where people are undressing or nude
</Callout>

Even in public buildings (stores, offices), cameras must **not** be aimed at stalls, urinals, or changing areas. Violations can lead to severe criminal and civil penalties under video voyeurism laws.

### Audio Recording: The Bigger Challenge

**Audio recording laws are stricter than video** in nearly every state. Many states require **all-party consent** (everyone being recorded must agree), while others require only **one-party consent** (you can record if you're a participant).

- **All-party consent states**: California, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, Nevada, New Hampshire, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Washington (and others)
- **One-party consent states**: Most of the remaining states

**Best practice**: Avoid audio recording unless you have explicit, written consent from all parties. Many installers disable audio to sidestep compliance risks entirely.

### Signage Requirements

Several states mandate visible notice when surveillance is in operation:

- Some require signs **only if audio is recorded**
- Others require signs for **any video surveillance** in business or workplace settings
- Notice must be "clear and conspicuous" (usually posted at entrances)

Signage is not just a legal requirement—it's a strong defense against "I didn't know I was being recorded" claims.

## State-by-State Breakdown

Below are the key rules for major states. Laws change frequently; verify current statutes before each project.

  <TabsContent value="california">
    California has some of the nation's strictest privacy protections, combining its state constitution's right to privacy with comprehensive wiretap and invasion-of-privacy laws.

    **Video Surveillance**: Permissible in public areas and most private commercial spaces where there is no reasonable expectation of privacy. Prohibited in bathrooms, locker rooms, and areas where people are undressing.

    **Audio Recording**: **Two-party (all-party) consent** required. Hidden microphones in public spaces are illegal without notice. Best practice: disable audio entirely unless you obtain written consent from all recorded parties.

    **Signage**: Required when audio is recorded. Business surveillance generally requires posted notice of video recording as well.

    **Special Considerations**: California's constitution recognizes a privacy right that can create liability even in areas without explicit statutory protection. Employers must notify employees of any surveillance that could affect their privacy. Be cautious with cameras that can zoom into private residences.

    <Callout type="warning">
      California's damages for violations can include statutory damages per violation, plus punitive damages and attorney fees. Proceed with extreme caution.
    </Callout>
  </TabsContent>
  <TabsContent value="texas">
    Texas allows video surveillance with a legitimate business or security purpose, but imposes strict rules on audio and hidden cameras.

    **Video Surveillance**: Legal in public areas and private commercial spaces where no reasonable expectation of privacy exists. Must avoid bathrooms, dressing rooms, and similar private areas.

    **Audio Recording**: **One-party consent** for oral communications—but the party recording must be a participant in the conversation. Covert audio without being a party to the conversation likely violates the law.

    **Signage**: Not required for video-only surveillance. If audio is recorded, signage is required to provide notice.

    **Special Considerations**: Texas prohibits "improper photography or visual recording" in private areas (similar to video voyeurism). Use of hidden cameras in places like locker rooms is a state jail felony. Employers have broad latitude to monitor the workplace but should develop written policies and notify employees to avoid common-law privacy claims.

    **Bottom line**: Texas is installer-friendly but take care with audio. Disable it unless you have consent.
  </TabsContent>
  <TabsContent value="florida">
    Florida enforces strong privacy protections through its constitution and statutes, particularly around audio and employer surveillance.

    **Video Surveillance**: Permissible in areas without a reasonable expectation of privacy (public spaces, store floors, warehouse areas). Prohibited in bathrooms, dressing rooms, and other private areas.

    **Audio Recording**: **Two-party (all-party) consent** is required for oral communications. Florida's Supreme Court has held that confidentiality is presumed for in-person conversations, so consent must be obtained.

    **Signage**: Required for both video and audio surveillance in many business contexts. Signs must be conspicuous and inform individuals they may be recorded.

    **Special Considerations**: Florida employers must provide written notice to employees before conducting electronic monitoring (including video) in areas where employees have a reasonable expectation of privacy. The state constitution's privacy provision has been interpreted to protect against surreptitious surveillance. Statutory damages for violations can reach $5,000 per violation (or more if malicious).

    **Employer tip**: Provide written policies, post signs, and avoid audio unless you have all-party consent.
  </TabsContent>
  <TabsContent value="new-york">
    New York has a mix of statutory and common-law privacy rules, with specific workplace surveillance requirements.

    **Video Surveillance**: Legal in public and commercial areas, but must avoid places where privacy is expected (restrooms, changing rooms). The state's "video voyeur" law criminalizes surveillance in private areas.

    **Audio Recording**: **One-party consent** for oral communications—but to be safe, obtain consent from all parties when possible.

    **Signage**: New York's **Stop Work and Surveil (SWS)** law requires employers to provide **written notice** to employees **before** implementing video surveillance that could implicate privacy interests. The notice must describe the areas monitored and the purpose. Signage in business premises is also commonly required.

    **Special Considerations**: New York courts recognize claims for "publicity given to private life" and "intrusion upon seclusion." Employers must balance security needs with employee privacy—best practice is to develop a written surveillance policy and share it with employees. Cameras in break rooms or lounges are highly sensitive; avoid these unless absolutely necessary and with notice.

    **Local variations**: New York City and other jurisdictions may have additional rules—check local ordinances.
  </TabsContent>
  <TabsContent value="illinois">
    Illinois has the nation's most aggressive biometric privacy law (BIPA) and strict consent rules for audio and video.

    **Video Surveillance**: Legal in public areas and most workplaces. However, if using facial recognition or other biometric identifiers (iris scans, fingerprint, voiceprint), BIPA applies with strict consent, notice, and data retention requirements.

    **Audio Recording**: **Two-party (all-party) consent** for oral communications.

    **Signage**: Required when audio is recorded under the eavesdropping statute. For biometric collection (facial recognition), Illinois requires separate written notice and signed release.

    **Special Considerations**: BIPA is a private right of action with **statutory damages of $1,000-$5,000 per negligent or reckless violation** (per person per scan). Class-action risks are high. Even installing a camera that collects facial geometry without BIPA compliance can trigger liability.

    **Employer rules**: Illinois employers must notify and obtain written consent before using biometric surveillance in the workplace. Many employers avoid facial recognition entirely.

    <Callout type="warning">
      If your cameras include any AI-powered facial recognition, people counting, or emotion detection, assume BIPA applies—and get explicit written consent or disable those features in Illinois.
    </Callout>
  </TabsContent>
</Tabs>

## Employer/Employee Surveillance

Federal and state laws intersect in workplace monitoring. Key guidelines:

- **Federal**: The National Labor Relations Act protects certain employee concerted activities; surveillance that interferes can create liability. The Electronic Communications Privacy Act allows employer monitoring of company-owned systems but requires notice in some contexts.
- **State**: Many states follow the general rule that employers can monitor employees in the workplace **if there is no reasonable expectation of privacy** and **if employees are notified**. State wiretap/eavesdropping laws still apply to audio.

**Best practices for installers**:
1. Advise clients to develop a **written surveillance policy** distributed to all employees
2. Post **clear signage** in monitored areas
3. **Avoid audio** unless absolutely necessary and properly consented
4. **Do not place cameras** in restrooms, locker rooms, or designated break areas
5. If using **biometric features** (facial recognition, time clocks with fingerprint), comply with BIPA (IL) and similar state laws

## Front Door and Public Areas

Generally, homeowners and businesses can monitor **their own property** without consent, provided they:

- Point cameras at their own entrances, driveways, sidewalks adjacent to their property
- Do **not deliberately capture** into private areas of neighbors (bedrooms, bathrooms) through windows
- Respect public spaces (you can film the street, but not with intent to peer into private residences)

For businesses, monitoring parking lots, entrances, and sales floors is typically permissible, but again—**avoid audio** without consent.

## Conclusion: Best Practices for Installers

To stay on the right side of the law:

1. **Always disable audio** unless you have explicit, documented consent
2. **Post conspicuous signage** in jurisdictions that require it (and as a general best practice)
3. **Never install cameras** in areas where privacy is expected (bathrooms, locker rooms, bedrooms)
4. **Avoid facial recognition/biometrics** in Illinois (and consider opt-out in other states)
5. **Notify employees** in writing before implementing workplace surveillance
6. **Document client consent**—have them sign off on camera placement plans
7. **Stay current**—privacy laws evolve; subscribe to industry updates
