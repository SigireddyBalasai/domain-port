## What Makes a Camera NDAA-Compliant?

NDAA (National Defense Authorization Act) compliance for security cameras centers on **origins and supply chain transparency**. Cameras must not contain components from "covered" countries (primarily China) and should be manufactured or assembled in the United States or other approved nations.

Key criteria to verify:

<Callout type="info">

**Compliance Checklist:** - **Country of Origin**: Made/assembled in USA,
Taiwan, Mexico, India, or other non-covered countries - **Component
Sourcing**: No critical components (sensors, processors) from China -
**Manufacturer Transparency**: Clear documentation from the vendor about
origin - **Supply Chain Traceability**: Ability to trace components through
the production chain

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

**Important**: Always ask for a **Country of Origin certificate** or **Bill of
Materials (BOM)** from the vendor before purchasing—especially for budget
cameras where compliance claims may be less rigorously verified.

</Callout>

## Buyer's Guide: How to Verify NDAA Compliance on a Budget

When shopping for affordable NDAA-compliant cameras, be diligent. Budget manufacturers may cut corners on documentation.

**Red Flags to Avoid:**

- **No country of origin listed** – If the manufacturer won't state where the camera is made, assume it's from a covered country.
- **"Designed in USA" without "assembled in"** – Design origin doesn't equal manufacturing origin. The entire device must be made outside covered countries.
- **No BOM available** – Lack of willingness to share Bill of Materials suggests component sourcing issues.
- **Suspiciously low prices (&lt;$100 for 4MP)** – At this range, compliance claims are often dubious.

**Certifications to Look For:**

- **ITES (Information Technology Examination)** reports from the manufacturer
- **GSA Schedule** listings – Many GSA-approved products are NDAA-compliant
- **FedRAMP** authorization for cloud components (if applicable)
- **Country of Origin certificates** provided on request

**Where to Buy:**

- Reputable security distributors (Anixter, Graybar, ADI)
- Direct from manufacturers (or authorized dealers)
- Avoid generic Amazon/eBay listings unless the seller is an authorized distributor

**Questions to Ask Vendors:**

1. "Can you provide a signed Certificate of Origin for this model?"
2. "Is this camera assembled in a non-covered country? Which one?"
3. "Do you have a Bill of Materials showing no Chinese components?"
4. "Has this model been used in any federal projects? Can you provide references?"

## Installation Tips: PoE vs. Wireless

Most NDAA-compliant cameras, especially at this price point, use **Power over Ethernet (PoE)**. Here's what you need to know:

### PoE Advantages

- **Reliable power and data** over a single cable
- **Longer runs** (up to 100 meters)
- **Greater cybersecurity** – no wireless attack surface
- **More budget-friendly** PoE cameras typically cost less than wireless equivalents

### Wireless Considerations

- **Easy retrofits** in buildings without Ethernet infrastructure
- **Flexible placement** – move cameras as needed
- **Potential performance issues** if network is congested
- **Battery maintenance** for battery-powered models

### Mounting

- Use **outdoor-rated enclosures** for exposed installations
- **Route cables through conduit** to protect from tampering
- **Avoid pointing cameras at reflective surfaces** (pools, glass) to prevent IR glare
- **Test field of view** before final mounting – use a temporary setup

### Are NDAA-compliant cameras lower quality than Chinese-made ones?

Not necessarily. Many of the cameras listed above deliver image quality comparable to or better than budget Chinese models. The primary difference is manufacturing location and component sourcing documentation.

### Can I use NDAA-compliant cameras for private property?

Yes. NDAA compliance is a **requirement** for federal projects and many state/local government contracts, but there's no prohibition on using compliant cameras on private property. Many private organizations choose them for supply chain security.

### What about NVRs/DVRs? Do they also need to be NDAA-compliant?

Yes. Recording devices, network switches, and other infrastructure components should also comply if the entire system is to be used on a federal project. Check each component's origin.

### Is "Made in Taiwan" always NDAA-compliant?

Yes. Taiwan is not a "covered country" under the NDAA. Cameras manufactured in Taiwan with components not sourced from China are generally considered compliant, but always verify specific component sourcing if compliance is critical.

### What if I already installed non-compliant cameras?

For existing non-federally funded projects, replacement may not be mandatory. For federal projects or grants, consult with your contracting officer—some contracts allow phase-out timelines.
